# Relational Process Field Architecture

## 0. Position

This document defines a replacement architecture for DRAF.

DRAF starts from:

```text
Person A x Person B x Relationship x Environment
```

This architecture rejects that starting point.

Its core claim is:

```text
Persons are not primitive simulation units.
Persons are stabilized patterns produced by relational, embodied, historical, and institutional processes.
```

The simulation should therefore not begin with two complete people who then interact. It should begin with a field of processes that repeatedly stabilize into recognizable subject positions, interaction patterns, identities, and fate-like trajectories.

The proposed architecture is:

```text
RPF: Relational Process Field
```

Its purpose is to simulate:

```text
how relation-processes become recognizable as persons,
how those persons become mutually binding,
how repeated interaction produces identity,
and how irreversible history narrows possible futures into fate.
```

This is not a story generator. It is a generative model of human relational becoming.

## 1. Ontological Commitments

### 1.1 Person Is an Emergent Interface

`Person` is not a base entity. A person is a high-level interface produced by the temporary stabilization of:

- embodied regulation
- attention and relevance realization
- habitus and social position
- interaction rituals
- recognition demands
- memory and narrative continuity
- institutional classification
- material dependency
- repeated relational patterns

The system may expose a `Person View`, but internally it should not treat the person as the deepest unit.

### 1.2 Relation Is Not an Edge

A relation is not a variable-bearing link between two people.

A relation is a repeating process pattern that determines:

- who can affect whom
- who can define the situation
- whose suffering counts
- whose silence means what
- whose withdrawal triggers pursuit
- whose need becomes an accusation
- whose role must be maintained for the other to remain coherent

Relation is therefore a pattern of mutual production, not a connection.

### 1.3 Interaction Is Not Action Exchange

Interaction is not two agents choosing actions and responding to each other.

An interaction is a situated episode in which multiple orders become locally visible:

- material constraints
- social roles
- face-work
- second-order observation
- bodily regulation
- recognition struggle
- misrecognition
- irreversible consequences

The smallest dramatic unit is not an action. It is a situation in which something becomes necessary, impossible, dangerous, or unsayable.

### 1.4 Environment Is Not Background

Environment is not an external container.

It is a layered field of constraints and affordances that is partly objective, partly perceived, and partly enacted by previous relation-processes.

The field does not merely influence subjects. It participates in producing what a subject can be.

### 1.5 Fate Is Path Dependency

Fate is not a plot.

Fate emerges when local choices, repeated interpretations, social recognition, material dependency, and irreversible events progressively narrow the future.

```text
freedom exercised under constraint
-> repeated pattern
-> social recognition
-> irreversible history
-> reduced future possibility
-> fate-like trajectory
```

## 2. Core Unit: Relational Process Pattern

The minimal simulation unit is:

```text
RPP: Relational Process Pattern
```

An RPP is a historically situated pattern of difference reproduction.

It describes how a process stabilizes, triggers another process, receives interpretation, and becomes part of future structure.

### 2.1 RPP Schema

```text
RPP
- id
- name
- field_conditions
- participating_positions
- triggering_difference
- relevance_shift
- embodied_response
- communicative_form
- recognition_demand
- relational_effect
- stabilization_effect
- irreversibility_effect
```

Example:

```text
RPP: Pursuit-Withdrawal Loop

field_conditions:
  - one position has low exit capacity
  - one position has high autonomy sensitivity

triggering_difference:
  - delayed response
  - ambiguous silence

relevance_shift:
  - silence becomes evidence of abandonment

embodied_response:
  - vigilance, agitation, narrowing attention

communicative_form:
  - indirect demand, repeated checking, accusation disguised as concern

recognition_demand:
  - "prove that I matter"

relational_effect:
  - the other experiences contact as control

stabilization_effect:
  - one position becomes "needy"
  - the other becomes "avoidant"

irreversibility_effect:
  - future silence is interpreted through the established loop
```

The important point: "needy person" and "avoidant person" are not inputs. They are stabilized names for positions inside a recurring RPP.

## 3. Layered Architecture

RPF has nine layers.

They are not a simple pipeline. They are mutually constraining orders. Each episode samples from all layers at once, but the architecture can be described from deeper to more visible levels.

```text
1. Material Reproduction Layer
2. Field-Position Layer
3. Embodied-Habitus Layer
4. Relevance Realization Layer
5. Co-Presence Binding Layer
6. Interaction Ritual Layer
7. Communicative Observation Layer
8. Recognition and Misrecognition Layer
9. Stabilization and Irreversibility Layer
```

---

## 4. Material Reproduction Layer

This layer replaces psychological accounts such as safety, energy, and control.

It asks:

- Who depends on what to continue living?
- Who performs visible and invisible labor?
- Who restores whom?
- Who owns resources?
- Who controls time?
- Who can leave?
- Who pays the cost of repair?
- Who absorbs instability?

### 4.1 Core Structures

```text
Reproduction Structure
- resource_access
- labor_distribution
- care_obligations
- time_control
- bodily_capacity
- dependency_chain
- exit_capacity
- recovery_cost
```

### 4.2 Replacement Principle

Do not model "energy account" as an internal bar.

Model:

```text
who is depleted,
by which obligations,
with what recovery access,
and whose depletion is socially recognized.
```

### 5.3 Example

Two people may experience the same silence differently because their field positions differ.

For one, silence is privacy.

For the other, silence is a familiar sign of being excluded from power.

## 10. Communicative Observation Layer

This layer replaces a thin interpretation module.

It models second-order observation.

### 10.1 Communication Triad

Every communication event contains:

```text
information: what is selected as content
utterance: how and why it appears in this form
understanding: how the receiver distinguishes information from utterance
```

### 10.2 Second-Order Observation

Each position observes:

```text
the other,
the other's observation of self,
the other's observation of this observation,
and the scene's possible observation by absent audiences.
```

### 10.3 Core Structures

```text

- omission
- inferred_intent

- other_as_seen_seeing

```

### 10.4 Principle

Conflict often does not come from disagreement about content.

It comes from disagreement about what the communication means for the relationship.

## 13. Global Simulation Cycle

RPF does not use a person-centered loop.

It uses a scene-centered and process-centered cycle.

```text
1. Field Configuration
   Material, institutional, symbolic, spatial, and historical conditions define possible scenes.

2. Binding Evaluation
   The system determines whether and why positions remain co-present.

3. Scene Crystallization
   A concrete interaction scene forms around unresolved tensions, obligations, or accidents.

4. Relevance Shift
   Each position's attention and affordance landscape is reorganized.

5. Ritual Framing
   Roles, face risks, permissible speech, and audience structures are established.

6. Communication Event
   Utterance, omission, gesture, timing, silence, or action occurs.

7. Second-Order Observation
   Each position interprets not only what happened, but what the event implies about how it is seen.

8. Recognition Struggle
   Demands for recognition are granted, refused, displaced, mocked, postponed, or misunderstood.

9. Relational Process Update
   Recurring patterns are reinforced, weakened, inverted, or transformed.

10. Stabilization Update
    Patterns become more or less person-like.

11. Irreversibility Update
    Some consequences become part of the future's structure.

12. Field Reconfiguration
    The world that future scenes occur in has changed.
```

## 14. Dramatic Generation

Drama should not be generated from goals alone.

The basic dramatic generator is:

```text
incompatible requirements for maintaining recognizability, agency, and relation under constraint
```

### 14.1 Core Dramatic Contradictions

```text
Recognition Contradiction
I need you to see me, but being seen would expose what I must hide.

Dependency Contradiction
I need you, but needing you threatens the self I must maintain.

Position Contradiction
I must perform a role that prevents me from saying what the role costs.

Face Contradiction
The truth would repair the relation but destroy the scene.

Care Contradiction
Helping you keeps us bound in the very pattern that hurts us.

Exit Contradiction
Leaving would save me, but make my past sacrifices meaningless.

Memory Contradiction
To continue together, we must remember differently.

Class Contradiction
What you call love, I experience as dependence; what you call ambition, I experience as abandonment.

Identity Contradiction
To become what you ask, I must stop being the person you originally loved.
```

### 14.2 Tension Without Plot

Long-term tension emerges from:

- unresolved recognition demands
- binding without fusion
- repeated misrecognition
- unstable repair
- rising exit costs
- shrinking alternative futures
- incompatible self-continuities
- public identities that lag behind private change

## 18. Recursive Layered Aggregation

RPF is not a flat variable model. It is also not a simple bottom-up hierarchy.

It is a recursive layered architecture:

```text
Micro Signals
-> Generative Parameters
-> Process Patterns
-> Relational Aggregates
-> Person / Relationship Views
-> Narrative and Social Labels
-> Operative Classifications
-> Field and Interaction Constraints
-> Micro Signals
```

The system must distinguish between:

```text
surface concepts as observer summaries
```

and:

```text
surface concepts as socially operative facts
```

This distinction is critical. A label such as "cold", "dependent", "untrustworthy", or "controlling" must not directly cause behavior merely because the simulator computed it. But if that label is spoken, believed, institutionally recorded, socially repeated, or incorporated into self-narrative, it becomes part of the field and can constrain future behavior.

### 18.1 Layer 0: Micro Signals

Micro signals are concrete, low-level differences that may or may not become meaningful.

```text
Micro Signal
- delay
- tone_shift
- gaze_avoidance
- physical_distance
- fatigue_marker
- interruption
- omission
- repeated_phrase
- spatial_position
- object_use
- timing
- touch
- bodily_tension
```

These signals do not have fixed meaning. Their meaning depends on field position, history, relevance realization, and active RPPs.

Example:

```text
A delayed reply is not inherently rejection.
It becomes rejection only inside a relevance landscape where delay is already coupled to abandonment, exclusion, or punishment.
```

### 18.2 Layer 1: Generative Parameters

Generative parameters are deep structures that participate directly in simulation.

They are not personality traits. They are constraints on perception, action, recognition, and repair.

```text
Generative Parameter
- exit_capacity
- speech_inhibition
- shame_threshold
- threat_sensitivity
- face_risk_threshold
- relevance_trigger_weight
- repair_acceptance_threshold
- risk_suspension_scope
- recognition_dependency
- symbolic_capital_gap
- material_dependency_chain
- bodily_recovery_access
- action_reversibility
```

These parameters do not describe what someone "is". They describe what becomes available, dangerous, visible, costly, or impossible from a given position.

### 18.3 Layer 2: Process Patterns

Process patterns are recurring dynamic forms.

They are closer to causality than high-level traits.

```text
Process Pattern
- pursuit_withdrawal
- symmetrical_escalation
- complementary_dependency
- double_bind
- meta_communication_failure
- face_saving_loop
- recognition_pursuit
- risk_checking_loop
- repair_avoidance
- contribution_debt_loop
- silence_interpretation_loop
```

Process patterns are activated when micro signals encounter compatible generative parameters and field conditions.

Example:

```text
delay
+ high abandonment relevance
+ low exit capacity
+ prior unrepaired withdrawal
-> risk_checking_loop
```

### 18.4 Layer 3: Relational Aggregates

Relational aggregates are readable summaries of deeper patterns.

They may be useful for inspection, debugging, and narration, but they must not be treated as primitive causes.

```text
Relational Aggregate
- trust_view
- intimacy_view
- dependency_view
- resentment_pressure_view
- power_asymmetry_view
- repair_capacity_view
- conflict_pressure_view
- stability_view
- exit_asymmetry_view
```

Example:

```text
trust_view
= risk_suspension_scope
+ checking_frequency
+ vulnerability_exposure_permission
+ ambiguity_tolerance
+ betrayal_expectation
+ repair_recovery_speed
```

The simulator may say "trust is declining", but the state update must occur in the underlying structures.

### 18.5 Layer 4: Person and Relationship Views

Person View and Relationship View are projections.

They summarize stabilized process patterns in a human-readable form.

They answer:

```text
What kind of person does this position currently appear to be?
What kind of relation does this process currently appear to be?
```

They do not answer:

```text
What is the person's essence?
What is the relationship's true variable state?
```

### 18.6 Layer 5: Narrative and Social Labels

Narrative labels are surface descriptions used by observers, narrators, characters, and social fields.

```text
Narrative Label
- "cold"
- "needy"
- "loyal"
- "controlling"
- "ungrateful"
- "changed"
- "not like before"
- "in a cold war"
- "more like parent and child than lovers"
```

As observer summaries, these labels have no direct causal power.

As spoken or socially recognized classifications, they may become operative.

### 18.7 Layer 6: Operative Classifications

An operative classification is a label that has entered the causal field.

This happens when a label is:

- spoken inside an interaction
- accepted into self-narrative
- repeated by a third party
- recorded by an institution
- used to justify action
- used to deny recognition
- used to predict future behavior
- used to restrict possible repair

```text
Operative Classification
- label
- source
- target_position
- audience
- legitimacy
- uptake
- sanction_power
- identity_pressure
- future_interpretation_bias
```

Example:

```text
"You are cold."
```

As a mere observer label, this is a summary.

As an uttered accusation, it becomes:

- a face threat
- a relation definition
- a demand for recognition
- a possible imposed identity
- a future interpretation frame

It can increase speech inhibition, alter relevance triggers, reduce repair capacity, and stabilize the very pattern it names.

### 18.8 Downward Constraint

High-level labels do not magically cause behavior.

They affect lower layers only through specific channels:

```text
label
-> face risk
-> speech inhibition
-> relevance trigger weight
-> self-narrative pressure
-> action availability
-> future micro-signal interpretation
```

This is downward constraint, not direct trait causation.

### 18.9 Aggregation Rule

The system must obey this rule:

```text
No high-level concept may directly update behavior unless it has become operative in the simulated world.
```

Allowed:

```text
risk_suspension_scope decreases
checking_frequency increases
ambiguity_tolerance decreases
-> trust_view declines
```

Not allowed:

```text
trust -= 0.2
-> character becomes suspicious
```

Allowed:

```text
"You never trust me" is spoken
-> face risk rises
-> self-narrative pressure rises
-> repair threshold changes
-> future ambiguity becomes more charged
```

### 18.10 Historical Lock-In

Repeated aggregation and operative classification create lock-in.

```text
micro signal
-> repeated interpretation
-> process pattern
-> aggregate
-> label
-> operative classification
-> downward constraint
-> more compatible micro signals
```

This loop explains how a temporary pattern becomes a personality, how a misunderstanding becomes a relationship structure, and how a local scene becomes fate.

## 22. LLM Role

The LLM must not decide the plot.

Its role is:

- render scene description from current field and interaction state
- verbalize communication events
- narrate silence, gesture, omission, and timing
- expose only what the current perspective can notice
- produce human-readable summaries of process changes
- translate deep structures into dramatic surface

The LLM should not invent state transitions that are not licensed by the simulator.

## 24. Evaluation Criteria

The simulation is successful when it produces:

- recognizable persons that were not fully predefined
- recurring patterns that feel causally deeper than personality traits
- behavior that is constrained without feeling mechanically determined
- long-term relational tension without external plot forcing
- misrecognition that is structurally patterned
- repair that changes the relation without erasing injury
- environment that becomes internalized into perception and action
- identities that are socially and materially maintained
- fate-like outcomes that emerge from path dependency
- high-level concepts that emerge from lower layers rather than driving them directly
- labels that become causal only when socially or narratively operative

The simulation has failed when:

- character behavior can be explained by trait labels
- relationship variables drive outcomes directly
- environment acts only as event source
- conflict is mostly goal opposition
- memory is a passive database
- people remain separable from field position
- long-term encounters require arbitrary scheduling
- the LLM has to invent dramatic causality
- high-level aggregates such as trust, intimacy, or personality directly update behavior
- labels affect lower layers without being spoken, believed, institutionalized, or otherwise made operative

## 25. Short Architecture Summary

RPF replaces:

```text
Person -> Interaction -> Relationship -> Identity -> Fate
```

with:

```text
Field
-> Process
-> Coupling
-> Scene
-> Observation
-> Recognition
-> Stabilization
-> Person
-> Fate
```

More precisely, mature RPF uses recursive layered aggregation:

```text
Micro Signals
-> Generative Parameters
-> Process Patterns
-> Relational Aggregates
-> Person / Relationship Views
-> Narrative and Social Labels
-> Operative Classifications
-> Field and Interaction Constraints
-> Micro Signals
```

The central reversal is:

```text
Do not simulate how people form relationships.
Simulate how relational processes stabilize into people.
```
